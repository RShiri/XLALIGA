Standouts — Every Dot Is a Player

Pick a stat and see the whole league's distribution at once. The curve is how all players spread out, the dashed line is the average, and each dot is one player. The names way out on the right are the anomalies — the ones breaking from the pack.

How to read this

This is a density plot: where the curve is tall, lots of players sit on that value; where it's flat, hardly anyone does. Most players cluster near the average, so the curve peaks there and tails off. A player far to the right of the crowd is doing something rare — we measure exactly how rare in σ (sigma) over average: +1σ beats ~84% of players, +2σ beats ~98%, +3σ is a genuine outlier.

Dots are coloured pink once a player is 2σ or more above average. Pick a player to spotlight them in gold and see their percentile.

xG Efficiency Lab

Do teams score as much as the quality of their chances says they should? These charts compare expected goals (xG) with what actually happened.

New here? What is xG?

Expected goals (xG) rates every shot by how likely it was to be scored, from 0 to 1. A tap-in might be 0.7 xG; a hopeful 30-metre strike 0.03. Sum a team's shots and you get how many goals an average finisher would score from those chances — a better "who deserved it" number than the scoreline alone.

Where does the xG itself come from? We built the model ourselves. Every shot is scored by a logistic-regression model trained on 11,830 real shots — the whole 2025/26 La Liga season plus the 2026 World Cup — against what actually happened to them. It weighs the shot's distance, the angle the goal mouth opens up from that spot, whether it was a header, an Opta big chance, and the situation (open play, free kick, corner, set piece or fast break). A per-league finishing calibration then keeps summed xG in line with goals actually scored — penalties are fixed at 0.76. The same model runs across our La Liga, World Cup and FC Barcelona dashboards, so the numbers compare cleanly everywhere.

La Liga xG is computed from the deep-scraped WhoScored shot data. Until a match is deep-scraped it contributes to the table but not to these charts.

Did chances turn into goals?

Every dot is one team's performance in one match: how many goals an average team would expect from their chances (across →) versus how many they actually scored (up ↑). The dashed line is "scored exactly what the chances were worth". Above the line = clinical finishing; below = chances wasted. The green line is the overall trend.

How closely do goals follow xG?

If xG were perfect, goals would track it exactly. This measures how strong that link has been this season — and how much finishing skill or luck is adding noise on top.

The same story as a distribution — games by chance quality

Every team-match sorted into a bucket by how much xG it produced. The bar is how many games created that much chance quality; under each bar, avg xG → avg goals actually scored from those games. Green = those chances over-delivered, red = under-delivered, blue ≈ spot on. Most football lives in the low buckets — big-chance games are rarer, and this shows whether they were cashed in.

Finishing: goals scored minus xG

For each club, total goals minus total xG. Green = clinical (scored more than their chances were worth — sharp finishing or good luck); red = wasteful (scored fewer — poor finishing or unlucky). Around zero means they scored about what was expected.

Who's good at both ends? — attack vs. defence quality

Each club is placed by how dangerous its attack is (further right = more/better chances created per game) and how mean its defence is (higher up = fewer/worse chances allowed per game). The dashed lines are the league average, splitting the pitch into four types of team — the colour key below explains each. Top-right = strong both ways; bottom-left = leaky and blunt.

Are teams getting the points they deserve?

Expected points turns each game's xG into the points an average team would win from those chances. Compare it with real points: above the line = winning more than the performances merit (clinical or lucky); below = deserved more than they've got.

Quality vs quantity of shots

Shots per game (across →) vs average xG per shot (up ↑ = better chances). Top-right = lots of good chances; top-left = few but excellent looks; bottom-right = high-volume long-range shooting; bottom-left = struggling to create anything.

Is there a home advantage?

Average xG created by clubs playing at home vs away. A clear gap suggests the home side tends to create better chances — the classic home advantage, in chance-quality terms.

Team-by-team xG ledger

The full breakdown — click any column header to sort. xG = quality of chances created; xGA = quality of chances allowed; G−xG = attacking finishing (＋ = clinical); xGA−GA = goalkeeping/defending vs the chances faced (＋ = kept out more than expected); xGD = xG minus xGA, i.e. how much a team out-chanced opponents overall ("deserved" goal difference).

Did the team that "deserved" it actually win?

For every game with xG, we check whether the side with more xG (the better chances) got the result. A high match rate means xG is tracking reality; mismatches (✘) are the games that defied the underlying play — often decided by a worldie, a penalty, or a red card.

Unluckiest results (deserved more)

Games where the side that out-created their opponent by the most xG still failed to win — they made the better chances but didn't get the result. The biggest "should have won this" performances.

Season Projection

Where does the table finish? A Poisson model built from this season's goals (home/away attack & defence strengths) simulates the remaining fixtures thousands of times to estimate final points and the odds of the title, a top-four/European finish, and relegation.
